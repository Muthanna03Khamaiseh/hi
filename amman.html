• Roman amphitheater

    A Roman theater located in the eastern part of the Jordanian capital, Amman, specifically at the foot of Jabal Al-Joufeh, on one of the hills opposite the Amman Citadel. A Greek inscription on one of the pillars indicates that this amphitheater was built in honor of Emperor Madrian, who visited Amman in 130 AD. Next to the amphitheater is the Forum Square, and their combined area is 7,600 square metres. Its construction probably dates back to the second century AD, specifically between 138 AD and 161 AD during the reign of Caesar Anthony Pius. Roman amphitheater used for musical and theatrical performances. Because of the quality of its sound system, it is still used sometimes for artistic performances. The theater can accommodate 6,000 spectators, which is larger than the southern theater in Jerash.

  • Iraq Prince Palace

    It is an ancient palace located in Jordan, half a kilometer south of the town of Iraq Al-Amir, 35 kilometers west of Amman. It dates back to the Hellenistic period in the second century BC and was built by Hyrcanos of the Ammonite Tobias dynasty during the reign of King Seleucus IV. The Iraq Al-Amir Palace was given this name because its entrance resembles the door of the cave, and the Prince is Tobias. The palace was 38 meters long and 18 meters wide. The palace was surrounded by a basin that was accessed by water from nearby springs. This palace has two entrances: north and south; It consists of two floors, the lower floor of which was used for storage and guard rooms, while the second floor was not completed because Hyrcanus, who built it, committed suicide due to the threat of the Seleucid army to the area. The palace is distinguished by the statues that rise above it, of eagles and lions, some of them facing each other and some of them arching. This indicates the power of Hyrcanus.

After King Abdullah I succeeded in his negotiations with the British, and obtained British recognition of an independent government in Transjordan, King Abdullah I chose in 1924 AD a high place overlooking the city center of Amman to build the headquarters of the governor’s palace, and the new palace was named after Raghadan. meaning prosperity and contentment, and to be a source of hope and optimism; He also wanted the palace building design to be luxurious and aesthetics characterized by simplicity and in harmony with the great history of the city of Amman.

  • Al-Muwaqqar Palace and Al-Baraka

    The palace is distinguished by its wonderful decorations, as a number of column capitals were found in its location, which were elaborately carved in the Byzantine style, and a complete column was found with a beautiful kufic inscription on its crown. The huge stones that make up the column bear inscriptions at equal distances as evidence of the scales in calligraphy. The palace was built on top of Jabal Al-Muwaqar, which is 910 meters above sea level, and a series of terraces 65 meters long, 39 meters wide, and a wall thickness of approximately 1.15 meters were used in its construction. Four towers, two square and two semi-circular, and the palace had half-barrel domes similar to the domes of the nearby palaces, namely the brick, Omara and Al-Mashta.

  • Al Mashta Palace

    It is one of the Arab palaces built by the Umayyads in the Levant. The palace is located 32 km southeast of Amman. It was built by the Umayyad Caliph Al-Walid bin Yazid in 744 AD. The palace is surrounded by a square wall 144 meters long, with 25 circular towers, except for the two entrance towers, which are half octagonal. The palace is located in Jordan in the Giza district. The palace is divided into three wings. The middle wing is in turn divided into three sections, the northern and southern sections, comprising the main buildings. The middle section in the middle wing forms an open courtyard. The northern wing was the residence of the caliph, while the southern wing is corridors, rooms and a mosque, and from it the only entrance opens, which is decorated from the outside with an ornate facade. However, the most beautiful thing about Qasr al-Mashta from a technical point of view is the decoration carved in the limestone in the tribal facade in which the entrance is located. That facade was transferred to the Department of Islamic Arts in the Berlin Museum, and we see in Al-Mashta Palace some artistic elements that resemble the decorations of the Dome of the Rock.
